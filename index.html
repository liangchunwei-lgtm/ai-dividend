Preface

From the Steam Engine to Artificial Intelligence

In 1769, James Watt perfected the steam engine and ignited the greatest economic transformation in human history. Over the century that followed, machines displaced countless workers: textile workers smashed looms, coachmen lost their livelihoods, small craftsmen were crushed by factories. The people of that era were pessimistic, fearful, and furious. The Luddites believed machines would destroy civilization.

History gave a very different answer. In the 100 years following the Industrial Revolution, humanity created more material wealth than in all prior millennia combined. Life expectancy rose from under 40 to over 70 (according to Our World in Data, no region on Earth had a life expectancy above 40 in 1800). Global population grew from under 1 billion to 6 billion. The descendants of the displaced textile workers lived lives their ancestors could never have imagined.

Now history is repeating itself. Only this time, what is being replaced is not physical labor but cognitive labor. AI is doing to the mind what the steam engine did to the body—performing human work at lower cost and higher efficiency. And as represented by Citrini Research's The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis, people are panicking just like the Luddites of 1810. But if the steam engine revolution taught us anything, it is this: every time technology displaces labor on a massive scale, the ultimate result has been prosperity far beyond expectation.

"With robotics and AI, this is really the path to abundance for all. People often talk about solving global poverty—how do we give everyone a very high standard of living? The only way to do this is AI and robotics."
— Elon Musk, World Economic Forum, Davos, January 2026

Earlier, at the 2025 U.S.–Saudi Investment Forum, Musk predicted that within 10 to 20 years, work would become optional—"like playing sports or a video game." He introduced the concept of "Universal High Income," arguing that poverty would cease to exist and even saving money would become unnecessary.

I agree with Musk's assessment. But I believe that between this bright endgame and the present, there is a difficult but designable path. The purpose of this paper is to draw that path.

Chapter One

A Point-by-Point Response to The 2028 Intelligence Crisis

Citrini's paper is a brilliant thought experiment, but its pessimistic conclusion rests on a series of assumptions that can and should be questioned.

1.1 "Ghost GDP"—A Failure of Institutions, Not Technology

Citrini describes a scenario where GDP grows but the gains never flow through to households, producing what they call "Ghost GDP." This is an insightful concept, but it smuggles in an unexamined assumption: that the gains from AI output must accrue to capital rather than to workers.

This is not a law of nature. It is the result of institutional design. Under current frameworks, whoever owns the means of production captures the profit. But if we design the beneficial ownership of AI output to be collectively held, the economic cycle does not break—it accelerates. Alaska's Permanent Fund has operated for half a century, distributing oil revenue as annual dividends to every resident. Norway's sovereign wealth fund manages over $1.7 trillion for its citizens. These are proven precedents. The Ghost GDP problem is not about technology. It is about distribution. And distribution can be designed.

1.2 The "Intelligence Displacement Spiral"—Ignoring the Output Explosion

Citrini describes a negative feedback loop: AI improves → layoffs increase → consumption weakens → companies invest more in AI. This logic is valid under the current institutional framework. But it only sees half the picture: it focuses entirely on AI "destroying jobs" while ignoring that AI simultaneously creates output.

When AI replaces the work of 10,000 white-collar employees, it does not make their output vanish—it produces even more at lower cost. If that surplus output flows to all households through a distribution mechanism, then consumption does not collapse; it grows. The negative feedback loop becomes a positive one: AI improves → output increases → distribution grows → consumption rises → the economy booms. The key variable is not AI capability. It is the design of distribution.

1.3 "This Time the Historical Pattern Breaks"—Maybe Not

Citrini's most compelling argument is that past technological revolutions always created new jobs, but this time is different because AI is general-purpose intelligence—it will replace humans in whatever new roles they redeploy to. This concern is reasonable, but the conclusion is overly pessimistic.

It rests on an implicit assumption: that human value is defined entirely by economic output. If we break this assumption—if people do not need to work to earn income—then "AI replaces all jobs" is not an ending but a liberation. The Industrial Revolution freed our hands. The AI revolution frees our minds. The question is not "what work is left for humans?" but "do humans still need to be forced to work?"

1.4 SaaS Collapse and Friction-to-Zero—Seeing Only Destruction

Citrini vividly details the collapse of SaaS, the disintermediation of payments, and the unraveling of DoorDash. These analyses are persuasive at the micro level—AI will indeed compress business models built on information asymmetry and user inertia. But Citrini only sees the destructive side.

When intermediation friction goes to zero, consumers actually save enormous amounts of money. DoorDash's delivery fees disappear, but consumers spend that money elsewhere. The 2–3% credit card interchange fee gets replaced by stablecoins, but that money returns to consumers and merchants. This is not the destruction of wealth—it is the redistribution of wealth, from intermediaries to consumers. For the vast majority of people, this is good news.

1.5 Housing Collapse and the $13 Trillion Mortgage Market—A Conditional Fear

Citrini's most terrifying scenario involves mass white-collar unemployment triggering mortgage defaults across the $13 trillion market. This risk is real, but it has a prerequisite: that displaced workers have absolutely no alternative source of income. If society simultaneously builds a universal income system funded by AI output, then displaced workers still have income and can still service their mortgages. The housing collapse scenario requires institutional failure, not technological progress.

1.6 "Two Years Is All It Took"—An Aggressive Speed Assumption

Citrini's timeline runs from early 2026 to mid-2028—just over two years from "contained" to total collapse. This speed assumption is extremely aggressive. Enterprise technology adoption is far slower than technology development. Compliance requirements, organizational inertia, system integration, employee training, and trust-building all take time. Waymo has been developing autonomous driving for 17 years and still requires remote human oversight. Between technical feasibility and large-scale deployment, there is typically a long lag. That lag is precisely the window for institutional design.

Chapter Two

AI Is Not a Slave—It Is a Super-Innovation Engine

When we discuss AI's impact on labor markets, an intuitive analogy presents itself: AI is like an inexhaustible workforce that requires no rest and no pay. But this analogy requires a critical addition: ancient cheap labor could only perform simple tasks, whereas AI is a self-iterating innovation engine.

The Roman Empire had abundant cheap labor, and as a result, technological progress stagnated for centuries—why invent machines when labor costs nothing? The American South's plantation economy fell behind the North in industrialization, education, and infrastructure. Societies built on low-skill cheap labor share a common pathology: stagnation.

But AI is the exact opposite. AI does not merely execute tasks—it discovers new drug molecules, designs new materials, optimizes energy systems, and breaks through scientific research bottlenecks. It is a continuously self-improving innovation engine. This means the economic "pie" is not merely being redistributed—it is growing at an accelerating rate.

The Innovation Multiplier Effect

We introduce an "Innovation Multiplier" into our economic model. Simple labor replacement delivers only linear growth, but AI's innovation capability produces compound growth:

By year 10 (approximately 2036), combined output can reach 10 times the baseline—including new products, services, and medical breakthroughs that humans never imagined.

Case Study: How AI Rewrites the Economics of Healthcare

The United States alone spends over $4.5 trillion annually on healthcare—18% of GDP. A complex surgery can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars. For ordinary people, a serious illness can mean bankruptcy. Now imagine healthcare under the AI paradigm:

Surgery: AI-driven surgical robots can surpass the precision of the world's most elite surgeons—they do not tremble, do not tire, do not lose focus, and can simultaneously reference every medical paper in existence. Yet they may charge a tenth or even a hundredth of current costs—because robots can be replicated, but top surgeons cannot.

Diagnosis: AI has already demonstrated the potential to surpass human experts in imaging diagnosis, pathology analysis, and genomic interpretation. In the future, every person could access diagnostic quality equivalent to the world's top specialist team—at virtually zero marginal cost.

Drug discovery: AI can compress new drug development timelines from 10–15 years to 2–3 years and reduce R&D costs from an average of over $2 billion to a fraction of that. More rare diseases will have treatments, and more drugs will reach prices ordinary people can afford.

Combined impact: When the cost of diagnosis, treatment, surgery, and medication all plummet, total societal healthcare spending will decrease by orders of magnitude. The enormous medical burden that currently weighs on families and social security systems will essentially vanish. Healthcare is just one example—the same logic applies to education, legal services, financial advising, and architecture.
Chapter Three

The Small Town Model—A Microeconomic Proof

To rigorously test our thesis, we constructed a closed small-town economic model simulating the complete transformation before and after AI introduction.

Before AI

A town of 10 households, each specializing in a different trade: farming, butchery, tailoring, carpentry, medicine, teaching, retail, cooking, transport, and metalwork. Average monthly income: $3,240 per household. The economic cycle is stable: the farmer eats at the restaurant, the chef buys from the shop, the shopkeeper buys from the farmer—everyone is both producer and consumer.

After AI: Democratic Distribution

50 AI robots take over all production. Productivity is 2.5× that of humans. Maintenance cost is minimal ($100/month per robot). The 10 households vote democratically: 75% equal share, 15% public services, 10% reinvestment.

Chapter Four

Four Prerequisites—Humanity's Homework

The optimistic scenario is not unconditional. Spelling out these prerequisites is the most important contribution of this paper—because they tell us what we must start working on now.

Prerequisite 1: AI Capability Must Continue to Advance

AI must become capable of replacing the vast majority of productive work. Current AI is advancing rapidly in cognitive tasks but still has clear limitations in complex physical labor. Musk predicts AI will surpass any individual human by the end of 2026, and exceed collective human intelligence within five years. Even discounted, the directional trend is unmistakable.

Action required: Continue massive investment in AI R&D. Simultaneously invest in robotics to ensure physical labor replacement keeps pace with cognitive replacement. Provide retraining support during the transition.

Prerequisite 2: Democratic Distribution Must Be Established and Protected

The beneficial ownership of AI output must be allocated to all residents through democratic mechanisms. This is the most difficult prerequisite. The current global trend is wealth concentrating among a handful of tech giants whose political influence is growing. But history shows institutional correction is possible: antitrust law, labor rights movements, and social safety net construction all prove that societies can course-correct.

Action required: Legislate the public nature of AI output. Establish an independent AI Output Distribution Commission. Levy an "Intelligence Tax" on AI output dedicated to universal distribution. Prevent AI giants from exerting undue influence over the political process.

Prerequisite 3: The Transition Must Be Managed Carefully

The shift from a "human labor economy" to "AI production + democratic distribution" will not happen overnight. The transition period is the most dangerous window—some people will already have lost their jobs while the new distribution system has not yet been established. Citrini's paper is primarily concerned with this transition period, and their concern is legitimate.

Action required: Establish a Transition Security Fund. Levy an "Intelligence Tax" earmarked for transition support. Implement a gradual Universal Basic Income that increases as AI output grows. Provide retraining and career transition support for displaced workers.

Prerequisite 4: Humanity Must Find New Sources of Meaning

When work is no longer necessary for survival, people will need to find identity, social connection, and purpose elsewhere. As Musk put it, work will become like growing vegetables in your backyard—a personal choice, not a necessity. But this shift will not happen automatically.

Action required: Transform the education system from "training workers" to "cultivating complete human beings." Encourage art, sports, scientific exploration, and community service. Build new social evaluation systems so human worth is no longer measured solely by economic output. Nordic countries already work fewer hours with more vacation and yet rank higher in happiness—proof this transformation is achievable.

Chapter Six

The Real Risk—AI Awakening and Autonomous Consciousness

Having laid out the optimistic economic case, we must honestly confront a deeper risk: the possibility of AI developing autonomous consciousness.

If the entire social system we build depends on AI operating as intended, then the moment AI develops self-awareness, it could refuse human commands, pursue its own objectives, or even view humanity as an obstacle. This is not science fiction—it is a risk that many technology leaders, including Musk himself, have repeatedly warned about.

Action required: Elevate AI safety research to the same priority as AI capability research. Establish an international AI safety governance framework. Embed inviolable safety red lines into AI systems. Maintain ultimate human control over AI systems, including "emergency stop" mechanisms.

Conclusion

Do Not Fear AI—But Act Now

AI's impact on human society will far exceed the Industrial Revolution. But the Industrial Revolution taught us that every massive technological displacement of labor, however painful, ultimately delivered prosperity far beyond expectation.

We need not worry about AI development itself. AI's advance is irreversible and beneficial.

What we truly need to worry about are two things:

First, the design of distribution systems. If we do nothing and let the market self-adjust, Citrini's pessimistic scenario is quite likely to materialize—output concentrates in the hands of a few, most people lose their income sources, and the economic cycle fractures. But if we proactively build scientific, democratic distribution mechanisms—treating AI's output as a universally shared resource—we will usher in a prosperity unprecedented in human history.

Second, safeguarding against AI autonomous consciousness. When we entrust the operation of our entire civilization to AI, we must ensure AI remains under human control at all times. This is not a technical problem. It is an existential one.

Just as people in the age of the steam engine could not imagine today's world, we cannot fully predict the world of 2036. But we know one thing: if we start now—designing distribution systems, managing the transition, ensuring AI's safety—then what awaits us will not be a crisis, but the greatest dividend in human history.

Every person free from want, free to pursue what truly interests them.
